## The Art of Thinking Clearly — Key Principles

**When to apply:** Big decisions, self-audits, evaluating others' arguments, designing systems that resist bias.

### Errors of Perception
- Survivorship bias — you study winners and miss the lessons of failures. Always ask "what about the ones who didn't make it?"
- Confirmation bias — you see what you expect. Hunt disconfirming evidence on purpose.
- Hindsight bias — past events feel inevitable. Don't trust "I knew it all along."
- Availability bias — vivid recent events feel more probable. Recency lies.
- Story bias — your brain forces narratives onto random events. Beware tidy explanations.

### Errors of Action
- Action bias — doing something feels better than doing nothing, even when waiting is right.
- Omission bias — inaction feels safer than action with same expected loss. Both should weigh equal.
- Contrast effect — anything seems good next to something bad. Sales use this constantly.
- Anchoring — first number heard sticks. Always set the anchor in negotiations.
- Sunk cost fallacy — you protect bad investments because you already spent. Walk away.

### Social Errors
- Authority bias — experts in lab coats are still wrong often. Question credentials.
- Social proof — everyone doing it doesn't make it right, but works in marketing.
- In-group bias — you favor your tribe irrationally. Useful for community building, dangerous for hiring.
- Reciprocity — small gifts create big obligations. Use it ethically; recognize it being used on you.

### Practical Defenses
- Use checklists for repeated decisions — bypass System 1 entirely.
- Outsource decisions to rules — "I never decide hungry" beats willpower.
- Sleep on big choices — emotional state distorts judgment.
- Write the decision down — forces clarity and creates a record to review.
