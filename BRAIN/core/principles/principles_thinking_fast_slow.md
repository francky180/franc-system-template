## Thinking, Fast and Slow — Key Principles

**When to apply:** Big decisions, copy that needs to land, debugging your own thinking, evaluating data, designing user flows.

### Two Systems
- System 1 is fast, emotional, lazy — System 2 is slow, deliberate, expensive. Most decisions are System 1 disguised as logic.
- When stakes are high, force System 2 — write it down, sleep on it, get a second opinion.
- Cognitive ease creates belief — clean fonts, simple words, repetition all make claims feel true. Use this in copy.

### Common Biases to Watch
- Anchoring — the first number sets the frame. In pricing: anchor high, then offer the "deal."
- Availability — what comes to mind feels more likely than it is. Recent vivid events distort risk assessment.
- Loss aversion — losses hurt 2x more than equivalent gains feel good. Frame offers as "stop losing X."
- Confirmation bias — you find what you look for. Actively hunt evidence against your hypothesis.
- Halo effect — one good trait colors all others. Don't let charm fool you on competence.

### Decision Hygiene
- Pre-mortem before committing — imagine the project failed; list why. Catches blind spots.
- Replace narrative with base rates — "what % of startups like this succeed?" beats "I have a feeling."
- Beware WYSIATI (what you see is all there is) — actively ask what's missing from the picture.
- Substitute hard questions for easier ones unconsciously — catch yourself doing this.

### Copy & Persuasion Lessons
- Familiar = trustworthy. Repeat key claims across the funnel.
- Use round numbers for emotion, precise numbers for credibility.
- Vivid concrete examples beat statistics every time.
- Frame matters more than facts — "90% survival" sells better than "10% mortality."
