## The Black Swan — Key Principles

**When to apply:** Risk planning, evaluating "stable" situations, designing for the unknown unknown.

### What is a Black Swan
- Rare, high-impact, retrospectively predictable. Nobody saw it coming, then everyone "knew" it would happen.
- They drive most of history's change, yet our models pretend they don't exist.
- Examples: 9/11, the 2008 crash, COVID, the rise of the internet.

### Why We Miss Them
- Confirmation bias — we look at confirming data, not data that would disprove our model.
- The narrative fallacy — we force tidy stories on chaos.
- Silent evidence — we ignore everything that didn't survive to be measured.

### Build for Black Swans
- You can't predict them. You can only prepare to survive them.
- Avoid fragile structures — anything that fails catastrophically under stress.
- Have optionality — many small bets that limit downside and enable upside.

### Two Worlds
- Mediocristan — domains where individual events don't dominate (height, weight, salary). Bell curves work here.
- Extremistan — domains where one event dominates (book sales, wealth, market crashes). Power laws rule.
- Most important domains are in Extremistan. Don't apply Mediocristan thinking there.

### Practical Defense
- Don't trust the expert in domains driven by black swans. Their track record is luck.
- Treat any model that didn't predict a real-world black swan with suspicion.
- The strategy: be hyper-conservative about big losses, hyper-aggressive about small bets with huge upside.
- Position yourself to benefit from chaos rather than be destroyed by it.
