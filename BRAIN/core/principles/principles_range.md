## Range — Key Principles

**When to apply:** Career strategy, hiring, deciding what to learn, working in complex domains.

### Generalists Win in Complexity
- In stable, narrow domains (chess, golf), early specialization wins.
- In complex, changing domains (most of modern work), generalists win.
- The world is increasingly complex. Range matters more.

### Sampling Period
- Top performers often sampled many fields before specializing.
- Late starters with broad backgrounds often outperform early specialists.
- Don't panic if you haven't found your one thing. The sampling is the work.

### Analogical Thinking
- The best problem solvers borrow from outside their field.
- Cross-domain transfer creates breakthroughs that specialists can't see.
- Read widely. Cultivate fields outside your job.

### Slow Learning Beats Fast
- Cramming feels productive. It doesn't stick.
- Spaced repetition, struggle, and varied practice build durable skills.
- Embrace the slow path even when it feels inefficient.

### Wicked vs Kind Environments
- Kind environments — clear feedback, consistent rules. Specialists thrive.
- Wicked environments — feedback is delayed, biased, or absent. Generalists thrive.
- Most modern problems are wicked. Plan accordingly.

### Practical Rules
- Don't fear the meandering path. It's often the path of the most adaptive.
- Build T-shaped skills — deep in one, broad across many.
- Borrow from other industries. The answer often lives outside your field.
- Match strategy to environment. Don't apply specialist rules to generalist problems.
