## The E-Myth Revisited — Key Principles

**When to apply:** Building service businesses, systemizing operations, escaping owner-dependence.

### The Technician's Trap
- Most small businesses are started by technicians (good at the work) who suddenly own a business they don't know how to run.
- Doing the work and running the business are different jobs requiring different skills.
- Don't romanticize being the doer. Doers stay small.

### Three Roles
- Technician — does the work.
- Manager — organizes the work.
- Entrepreneur — visualizes and creates the future.
- Most owners are 90% technician and starve the other roles. Imbalance kills.

### Work ON the Business, Not IN It
- Build the business as if it were going to be franchised — even if you never franchise it.
- Every process must work without you. If only you can do it, you're the bottleneck.
- Document, systemize, train.

### The Franchise Prototype
- Imagine you're building one prototype location that will be replicated 5,000 times.
- That mindset forces clarity, consistency, and systemization.
- Even a one-location business benefits from this discipline.

### Practical Build
- Develop your business systematically: primary aim → strategic objective → org strategy → management → people → marketing → systems.
- Hire to systems, not to skills. Systems run; people manage systems.
- A business that depends on the owner's heroism is a job, not a business.
- The goal is to build a business worth selling, even if you never sell it.
