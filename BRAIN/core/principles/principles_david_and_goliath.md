## David and Goliath — Key Principles

**When to apply:** Competing against bigger players, leveraging weakness, finding asymmetric advantages.

### Apparent Strengths Are Weaknesses
- Goliath's size made him slow. His armor made him blind. His reputation made him overconfident.
- The biggest player is often the easiest to beat — if you fight on different terms.
- Don't envy size. Find what it costs them.

### Apparent Weaknesses Are Strengths
- David's small size made him fast. His sling was longer-range than Goliath's sword.
- Reframe your "disadvantages" as advantages.
- Underdogs win by changing the game, not by playing the giant's game better.

### Choose the Right Battle
- Don't accept the rules the big player defines. Make your own rules.
- Compete where the giant can't follow.
- Niches, speed, agility, focus — these are underdog weapons.

### The Inverted U
- More resources help up to a point — then they hurt. Too much money, too much support, too much advantage all create new problems.
- A sweet spot exists. Past it, more is less.
- Having less can force the discipline that wins.

### Desirable Difficulties
- Some obstacles are gifts. They force adaptation that becomes your edge.
- Dyslexics overrepresented among entrepreneurs — the difficulty taught compensation skills.
- Hardship can be the best teacher.

### Practical Rules
- Don't try to match the giant. Outflank them.
- Use what you have, not what you wish you had.
- The underdog who refuses to fight by the rules of the favored is the underdog who wins.
